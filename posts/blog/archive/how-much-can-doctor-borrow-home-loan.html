Why doctors can borrow more

Lenders view medical professionals as low-risk borrowers. That's built into how certain lenders structure their credit policy — doctors have high income stability, strong long-term earning trajectories, and very low historical default rates.

This translates into two practical advantages: higher borrowing multiples than standard applicants at the same income level, and access to LMI waivers at up to 90–95% LVR depending on the lender. A doctor on $180,000 a year typically has more options than someone on $180,000 in another profession.

These figures are general illustrations based on common lender benchmarks as at mid-2026. Your actual borrowing capacity depends on your full financial position, income type, existing debts, and the lender's assessment criteria — all subject to individual credit assessment.

How income type affects your borrowing

Salaried / employed

Easiest to assess. PAYG payslips and group certificates are sufficient. Overtime and on-call loadings can often be included if consistent over 12 months.

Locum / contracting

Lenders vary significantly. Some require 2 years ABN history; specialist medical lenders may accept 6–12 months with agency confirmation of ongoing work.

Practice owner

Business income requires 2 years of tax returns and financials. Lenders use a net profit figure and may add back some expenses. A broker is essential here.

Registrar / intern

Many registrars qualify for competitive loans. Hospital contract = assessed like an employed borrower. Some specialist lenders consider future income trajectory.

$20K–$30K

Typical LMI saving at a $1M purchase

Most borrowers need a 20% deposit to avoid Lenders Mortgage Insurance. Eligible doctors can access LMI waivers at up to 90–95% LVR with certain lenders — meaning a 5–10% deposit can be enough to avoid the premium entirely.

On a $1,000,000 purchase, LMI at 90% LVR would typically cost $20,000–$30,000. That's either saved outright, or allows you to enter the market sooner. Subject to lender eligibility and individual credit assessment.

Factors that reduce borrowing capacity

  • HECS/HELP debt — for a doctor on $150K, HECS repayments can reduce borrowing capacity by $80K–$120K depending on the lender
  • Existing debts — personal loans, car loans, and credit card limits all reduce capacity. Even unused card limits count
  • Living expenses — lenders use either your declared expenses or a benchmark, whichever is higher
  • Dependants — each child typically reduces borrowing capacity by $40,000–$60,000 depending on the lender
  • Investment property — rental income is partially credited, but existing investment loan repayments reduce serviceability

Frequently asked questions

Can a doctor buy without a 20% deposit?

Yes — eligible doctors can typically access LMI waivers at up to 90–95% LVR with certain lenders, meaning as little as a 5–10% deposit may be sufficient to avoid LMI. Eligibility depends on your situation and the lender's specific criteria, subject to credit assessment.

Does HECS-HELP debt affect my borrowing capacity?

Yes, significantly. Lenders treat HECS as a reduction to your disposable income. For a doctor on $150,000, HECS repayments can be $12,000–$15,000 per year — reducing borrowing capacity accordingly. A broker can show you the exact impact across different lenders.

Can I include overtime or on-call income?

In most cases yes, if consistent over at least 12 months. Lenders typically require payslips showing the income over a period, and some apply a discount (e.g. 80% of overtime counted). A broker can identify lenders that credit your specific income structure most favourably.

What documents do I need for a doctor home loan?

Standard: 2 recent payslips, last 2 years' group certificates or tax returns, 3 months' bank statements, evidence of deposit, and AHPRA registration details. Locum or self-employed applicants also need ABN registration and additional income evidence. A broker will prepare a full checklist before you apply.
