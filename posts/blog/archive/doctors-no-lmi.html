First — what is LMI?

LMI stands for Lenders Mortgage Insurance. It's a one-off fee that banks charge when you borrow more than 80% of a property's value. The catch? It protects the bank, not you — but you're the one who pays it.

On a $900,000 property in Melbourne with a 10% deposit, LMI can easily run into the tens of thousands of dollars. It's usually added to your loan, so you end up paying interest on it for the life of the loan too.

For most borrowers, LMI is unavoidable if you want to buy with less than a 20% deposit. But for doctors, the rules are different.

Why doctors can often skip LMI

A number of lenders have created special lending policies for medical professionals. The logic is straightforward — doctors have stable, high incomes and historically very low default rates. Lenders see them as low-risk borrowers and compete hard for their business.

Under these policies, eligible doctors can borrow up to 90% or even 95% of a property's value without triggering the standard LMI requirement. That means you can buy with a smaller deposit and avoid a fee that could otherwise set you back $20,000–$50,000.

In plain terms: instead of needing a 20% deposit to avoid LMI, you may only need 5–10%. The exact threshold depends on the lender and your circumstances.

Who qualifies?

Most no-LMI policies cover a broad range of medical professionals. Lenders vary, but typically this includes:

🩺

General Practitioners

GPs with full AHPRA registration are covered by most lenders offering this policy.

🏥

Medical Specialists

Surgeons, physicians, radiologists, anaesthetists and other registered specialists.

🦷

Dentists

Registered dentists and dental specialists are included with most lenders.

👁

Other Health Professionals

Optometrists, pharmacists and some allied health professionals, depending on the lender.

Full AHPRA registration is typically required. Some lenders will consider pre-registration candidates with a signed employment contract — useful if you're finishing your residency and want to get moving on a purchase.

LVR (Loan-to-Value Ratio) is just the loan amount expressed as a percentage of the property's value. A 90% LVR means you're borrowing 90% and contributing 10% as a deposit. You'll still need enough to cover stamp duty and other purchase costs on top of that.

What about self-employed doctors?

If you run your own practice or work as a contractor, lenders assess your income a little differently — usually requiring two years of tax returns and business financials. That doesn't disqualify you from a no-LMI policy, but it does affect which lenders are the best fit and what documents you'll need to pull together.

Several lenders with strong medical professional policies also understand self-employed medical income well, including income from mixed billing, practice ownership and locum work.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a 20% deposit as a doctor?
No. Eligible doctors can often borrow up to 90–95% LVR without paying LMI, meaning you may only need a 5–10% deposit (plus purchase costs like stamp duty).
What is LMI and why would I have to pay it?
LMI (Lenders Mortgage Insurance) is a fee lenders charge when you borrow more than 80% of a property's value. It protects the lender — not you — against the risk of default. On a $900K property at 90% LVR it can cost $15,000–$50,000 depending on the lender and loan size.
Do dentists qualify for no-LMI loans?
Yes — most lenders with a medical professional policy include registered dentists. See our guide: Do Dentists Qualify for a No-LMI Home Loan?
Can a medical intern or registrar get a no-LMI loan?
Some lenders will consider pre-registration applicants with a signed employment contract. See our full guide: Can a Medical Intern or Registrar Get a No-LMI Home Loan?
Is a no-LMI loan available for investment properties?
Yes — most lenders apply the same policy to investment purchases as well as owner-occupied. Conditions vary by lender.
What does AHPRA mean?
AHPRA is the Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency — the national body that registers and regulates health professionals in Australia. Full AHPRA registration is the standard proof of profession that lenders require to access medical professional lending policies.
