5 Signs You're Paying Too Much on Your Mortgage

Most Melbourne borrowers have no idea they're overpaying — because nobody tells them. Here are five clear signals your rate is costing you more than it should, and what to do about it.

Your mortgage rate isn't locked in — it's negotiable, and most borrowers who refinance save between $200 and $800 per month. These five signs will tell you whether it's time to act.

1
Sign One

Your Rate Hasn't Changed in More Than 2 Years

The lending market moves constantly. Lenders launch sharper rates to attract new customers — but they rarely drop your rate automatically. If you haven't reviewed or renegotiated in the last two years, your lender has almost certainly moved on without you.

When the RBA lifts rates, your rate goes up almost immediately. When the RBA cuts, your variable rate should come down — but not always by as much. This asymmetry is built into how lenders operate.

A borrower who last reviewed two years ago may be sitting 0.5–1.0% above where the market is today. On a $700,000 loan, that's $290–$580 per month.

What to do: Get a market comparison from a broker. It costs nothing, takes 15 minutes, and gives you a clear picture of what you should be paying versus what you are paying.

2
Sign Two

You're on Your Lender's Standard Variable Rate

The standard variable rate (SVR) is the default rate your lender reverts you to once an introductory or fixed period ends. It's almost always the highest rate on the lender's book — existing customers receive it; new customers receive something better.

If your last loan documents showed a fixed rate or a discounted introductory rate, and you've never renegotiated since that period ended, you are almost certainly on an SVR. Check your latest bank statement — it'll be on there.

This is the most common reason borrowers overpay. The fix is to either negotiate a discount directly with your lender (sometimes possible) or refinance to a more competitive product. A broker can do both.

3
Sign Three

Your LVR Has Improved But Your Rate Hasn't Moved

Lenders price risk. The more equity you hold relative to the property value, the less risk you represent — and that should translate into a lower rate. This is called loan-to-value ratio (LVR) tiering.

If you bought with a 10% deposit and are now sitting at 65–70% LVR after several years of repayments and capital growth, you likely qualify for a meaningfully sharper rate — but your lender won't automatically reprice you. You have to ask, or move.

  • Your property has grown in value since purchase
  • You've made 3+ years of principal repayments
  • You've made extra repayments or used an offset account
  • You started with LMI and now have more than 20% equity
4
Sign Four

You Have Features You're Paying For But Not Using

Offset accounts, redraw facilities, and package bundles all come with a price — usually built into a slightly higher rate. If you're on a $400 annual package fee with an offset account you've never loaded with savings, you're paying for something that isn't helping you.

This works the other way too: if you have a large offset balance or consistently park cash in offset, a package loan with offset could be saving you significantly on interest — and a bare-bones basic loan would cost you more overall despite the lower advertised rate.

Feature matching matters as much as rate matching. A 0.1% higher rate with a working offset can outperform a 0.1% lower rate without one — depending on your savings behaviour.

5
Sign Five

A Friend or Colleague Just Refinanced and Saved

This sounds informal — but it's actually a valid signal. If someone with a comparable loan size, property type, and income has just refinanced and is now paying noticeably less, the gap between your rate and what's available in the market has likely grown wide enough to act.

The loan market is competitive and conditions change. When multiple people around you are finding better deals, the window where your current rate was reasonable has probably passed.

  • Do you know your exact current interest rate right now?
  • Have you compared it to the current market in the last 12 months?
  • Do you know your current LVR and which tier it puts you in?
  • Has your financial situation improved since you took the loan?

If you answered no to any of these, a free mortgage health check is worth 15 minutes of your time.
