Fix Equifax Credit Report Errors Yourself

Save money with this step-by-step guide to disputing inaccurate information on your credit report.

What Are Credit Report Errors and Why They Matter

A credit report error is any incorrect or inaccurate information that appears on your Equifax credit file. This might be a misspelled name, a missed payment you actually made, accounts you don't recognise, or outdated information that should have been removed.

Credit report errors are more common than you might think. They can prevent you from getting approved for a home loan, car finance, or personal loan, even if your financial position is strong. In my case, a single misspelled letter in my surname meant my lender wouldn't approve my mortgage, despite my solid credit history. With a settlement date 60 days away and a substantial deposit at risk, I had two choices: pay a credit repair company $1,500 or do it myself.

The good news is that you don't need to pay a credit repair company. You can dispute Equifax errors yourself through their online portal at no cost. The only investment is your time and patience.

Why Fix Your Credit Report Now

If you're applying for a home loan, a credit report error can be the difference between approval and rejection. Lenders use credit reports to assess risk, and inaccurate information can hurt your chances even if it's not your fault.

Equifax is required by law to respond to disputes within 30 days. This might feel slow when you're on a tight timeline, but it's much faster than waiting months for a credit repair company to work their process.

The cost is zero. No ongoing fees, no hidden charges, no guarantee-but-keep-paying schemes. You lodge the dispute, you wait, and if Equifax agrees the information is wrong, they remove it.

Step-by-Step Guide to Disputing Your Equifax Report

Step 1: Get Your Current Equifax Report

Before you dispute anything, you need to see what's actually on your report. Visit equifax.com.au and request a free copy of your credit report. You're entitled to one free report per year. Review it carefully and identify the exact error you need to dispute.

Step 2: Access the Equifax Corrections Portal

Go to the Equifax Corrections Portal online. You'll see an option for "Credit Report Corrections" — click through and select whether you're disputing a personal or commercial credit report. In my case, I selected Individual Credit Report and then Individual Directly (since I was handling it myself).

Step 3: Accept the Terms and Conditions

Read through the terms carefully. Make sure you understand that by submitting a dispute, you're asking Equifax to investigate and correct the information. Having your credit report in front of you at this stage is essential — you'll need to refer to the exact details as they appear.

Step 4: Enter Your Personal Information Accurately

Type your details exactly as they appear in your government ID (passport, driver's licence, or birth certificate). Accuracy here is critical — if your personal details don't match Equifax's records, they may reject your dispute. If Equifax has employment history on file, make sure you enter that information correctly too.

Step 5: Select the Type of Error You're Disputing

The portal offers several dispute categories. For general issues like misspelled names or incorrect account details, select Consumer Credit Data. For commercial matters, select Commercial Credit Data. The second dropdown shows all dispute types: Enquiry, Default, Account Information, Identity Information, Repayment History, Court Writ/Default Judgement, Financial Hardship Information, Bankruptcy Correction, and Directorship/Proprietorship.

Keep in mind that while you can dispute multiple items, each requires evidence. For example, if you're disputing a default, you'll also need to contact the credit provider who listed it.

Step 6: State the Error Clearly and Professionally

This is your chance to make your case. Be professional, polite, and factual. Don't be angry or defensive — just state what's wrong and why. In my case, I explained the misspelling, noted the correct spelling from my government ID, and mentioned the urgency due to my settlement date. Keep it concise but clear.

Step 7: Upload Supporting Evidence

This step makes or breaks your dispute. Scan and upload documents that prove your case. For a name misspelling, upload copies of your passport, driver's licence, or birth certificate. For missed payments you actually made, upload bank statements or proof of payment. The stronger your evidence, the more likely Equifax will uphold your dispute.

Step 8: Submit Your Request and Track Progress

Submit the dispute. You'll receive a confirmation email from Equifax with a tracking number and a link to check progress online. Keep this tracking number safe. By law, Equifax has 30 days to respond. You may receive a follow-up email asking for more information or requesting an extension — this is normal.

Step 9: Receive Your Outcome

Equifax will send you a final response email with a password-protected PDF attached. This document confirms whether they've agreed to correct the information, partially corrected it, or rejected your dispute entirely. In my case, my initial request was lodged on 6 August and the response came through on 4 September — 29 days in total.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Submitting without a copy of your actual credit report. You need to know exactly what's listed.
  • Being vague about the error. State the exact incorrect information and the correct information side by side.
  • Forgetting to upload evidence. Equifax is more likely to uphold disputes with supporting documents.
  • Disputing too many items at once. Focus on the errors that matter most first.
  • Not following up if you don't hear back within 30 days. Contact Equifax if their response is late.
