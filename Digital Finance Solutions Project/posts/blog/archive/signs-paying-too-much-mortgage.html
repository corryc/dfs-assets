The loyalty tax is real

Lenders routinely offer their lowest rates to new customers. Existing borrowers who don't ask — or don't know to ask — often sit on rates 0.5%–1.5% above what's currently available. On a $600,000 loan, that's $3,000–$9,000 a year in unnecessary interest.

Here are seven signs your home loan has become uncompetitive. If you recognise more than two, a review is worth your time.

The 7 warning signs

1

Your rate starts with a 6 — or higher

Owner-occupier variable rates from competitive lenders are currently lower than many borrowers expect — and significantly lower than the rates many existing borrowers are sitting on. If your rate feels high, it's worth checking. The gap adds up quickly on large balances. (Rate ranges shown in this article are indicative market benchmarks only — not advertised product rates.)

2

You haven't reviewed your loan in 2+ years

The market moves. Rates change, lenders compete for market share, and new products emerge. A loan that was competitive in 2023 may no longer be. Review at least once a year.

3

You're paying for features you don't use

Professional packages typically cost $350–$400 per year and bundle offset accounts, credit cards, and rate discounts. If you're not using the offset or the card, you may be paying for nothing.

4

Your fixed rate is about to expire

Borrowers rolling off fixed rates often land on the lender's revert rate — which can be significantly higher than what new customers receive. If you're within 3 months of your fixed rate expiring, now is the time to act.

5

Your LVR has dropped below 80% but your rate hasn't improved

Lenders typically offer better rates as your loan-to-value ratio falls. If your property has grown in value or you've paid down the loan, you may qualify for a lower rate — but your lender may not volunteer that.

6

You've never called your lender to ask for a better rate

Lenders have discretion to reprice existing loans. Many borrowers who call and ask — quoting a competitor rate — get a discount within 24 hours. If you've never asked, you've potentially been overpaying every month.

7

Your income has grown but your loan terms haven't changed

Higher income and better credit history can make you eligible for products you didn't qualify for at the time of your original loan. A review can identify whether refinancing to a better product makes sense now.

What does switching cost?

A basic variable-to-variable refinance typically costs $800–$1,000 all up — covering the discharge fee from your current lender plus settlement with the new one. If you're on a fixed rate, break costs can be significantly higher depending on your remaining fixed term and the rate differential at the time.

On most refinances, switching costs are recovered within 3–6 months from the rate savings. A broker can calculate your break-even point before you commit to anything.

A mortgage review costs nothing and takes about 15 minutes. You don't need to refinance to get value — sometimes calling your lender with a better rate in hand is all it takes.

Frequently asked questions

How do I know if my rate is competitive?

Compare your current rate to what's available from competitive lenders now. If there's a gap of 0.3% or more, it's worth investigating. A broker can show you a direct comparison based on your actual loan size and LVR.

How long does refinancing take?

A standard variable-to-variable refinance typically takes 3–6 weeks from application to settlement. The process involves a new application, valuation, and discharge of your existing loan. A broker manages the paperwork and lender communication throughout.

Will refinancing hurt my credit score?

Every credit application creates an enquiry on your file, which has a small short-term impact. This is usually minor and temporary. A broker can help you avoid unnecessary applications by matching you to the right lender before submitting.

Can I get a better rate without refinancing?

Sometimes yes. Calling your lender and asking for a rate reduction — especially with a competitor rate in hand — often results in a discount without changing lenders. Worth trying first. If they won't move, refinancing is the next step.
