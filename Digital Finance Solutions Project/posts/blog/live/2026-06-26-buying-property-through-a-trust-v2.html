Buying Property in a Trust in Australia (2026)

What you need to know about trust structures, borrowing, and the major tax changes proposed in the May 2026 Federal Budget — before you decide.

7 min read Digital Finance Solutions Updated June 2026

Trusts have long been used by Australian property investors to protect assets and distribute income. But the landscape changed significantly in May 2026. The Federal Budget introduced proposed tax reforms that affect how trusts are taxed, how investment losses work, and what the long-term CGT position looks like. If you're considering buying property through a trust — or already hold property in one — here's what's current.

What Does It Mean to Buy Property in a Trust?

When you buy property in a trust, the trust itself owns the property — not you personally. You are either the trustee (the person who manages it) or a beneficiary (someone who benefits from the income or capital).

Discretionary (family) trust

The trustee has discretion over how income and capital are distributed among beneficiaries. Traditionally used for tax planning — distributing income to lower-income family members to minimise overall tax. The proposed 2026 Budget reforms target this structure specifically.

Unit trust

Beneficiaries hold fixed units, similar to shares. Income and capital are distributed proportionally. Generally not subject to the proposed discretionary trust minimum tax. Often used in commercial property or joint investment structures.

The tax strategy differences between these two structures matter a great deal in 2026. Speak to your accountant before choosing one — the rules are changing.

Can a Trust Borrow Money to Buy Property?

Yes — but the borrowing process is different from a standard personal home loan, and fewer lenders offer it.

When a trust borrows to purchase property, the trustee takes out the loan on behalf of the trust. Most lenders require the trustee to personally guarantee the debt. This means your personal credit position and income are still assessed — the trust structure doesn't insulate you from that.

What lenders typically look for

  • A copy of the trust deed — lenders need to verify the trust structure and trustee powers
  • Personal guarantee from the trustee (individual or corporate trustee directors)
  • Standard income and serviceability assessment — same as a personal loan
  • LVR typically capped at 80% — no LMI available for trust structures at most lenders
  • Signed trustee declaration confirming authority to borrow

80% LVR is a common cap — a 20% deposit is generally required. Exact eligibility depends on the lender, loan size, and trust type.

The 2026 Budget Changes — What You Need to Know

The May 2026 Federal Budget introduced three proposed measures that directly affect property held through a discretionary trust. These are not yet law — but they are significant enough that any investor should understand them before proceeding.

Budget Proposal — Not Yet Law

The following measures were announced in the May 2026 Budget. As of June 2026, legislation has not passed. Seek advice from your accountant before making structural decisions based on these proposals.

1. Proposed 30% minimum tax on discretionary trusts (from 1 July 2028)

A 30% minimum tax would apply at the trustee level on all trust income — including rental income from investment properties. The ability to distribute income to low-income beneficiaries to reduce overall tax would be significantly curtailed. Unit trusts and fixed trusts are excluded, as are complying superannuation funds, charitable trusts, and deceased estates.

2. Negative gearing quarantined for new purchases (from 12 May 2026)

Any residential investment property purchased after Budget night — including through a trust — can no longer offset rental losses against other income. Losses are quarantined to future property income or capital gains only. Properties purchased before 12 May 2026 are fully grandfathered. New builds remain eligible for standard negative gearing treatment.

3. CGT discount replaced with indexation (from 1 July 2027)

The 50% CGT discount would be replaced by an indexed cost base calculation, with a 30% minimum effective tax rate on all capital gains regardless of hold period. This changes the long-term exit calculation for trust-held property materially.

Pros and Cons of Buying Property in a Trust

Weighing up the full picture — including the proposed 2026 changes — here's how the trust structure stacks up today.

Potential advantages
  • Asset protection — separate from personal assets
  • Estate planning flexibility
  • Income distribution options (subject to proposed min tax)
  • Pre-12 May 2026 properties grandfathered
Disadvantages
  • Proposed 30% minimum tax reduces distribution advantage
  • Negative gearing quarantined for new purchases
  • Fewer lenders — not all accept trust borrowers
  • No FHOG or first home buyer stamp duty concessions
  • Setup and ongoing compliance costs
  • LVR capped at 80%

Who Does a Trust Structure Still Make Sense For?

The case for using a discretionary trust to buy new residential investment property has weakened for most investors. That said, there are still situations where a trust can make sense:

  • Investors already holding property in a trust purchased before 12 May 2026 — grandfathered under current rules
  • Commercial property investors — negative gearing quarantine applies to residential only
  • Investors prioritising asset protection over tax efficiency
  • Estate planning scenarios where asset control across generations is the primary goal
  • Investors considering new builds — still eligible for negative gearing under the proposal
